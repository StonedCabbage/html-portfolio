DEUS EX MACHINA

The term deus ex machina is Latin for "god from the machine," originating from ancient Greek theater, where actors playing gods were lowered onto the stage using a crane (mechane) to resolve complex plot situations. It was first popularized by playwrights like Euripides, who frequently used it in tragedies such as Medea, where a chariot sent by the sun god Helios rescues Medea from her predicament. The device allowed playwrights to conclude stories quickly and dramatically, often producing astonishment in the audience. Deus ex machina is a plot device where an unexpected, external force abruptly resolves a seemingly unsolvable problem in a story.
